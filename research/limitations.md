# Limitations

## Purpose

Two-Set Policy is currently an early research hypothesis and framework.

This file records the main limitations that should be considered when interpreting the repository, its case studies, and its technical artefacts.

The purpose is to prevent the framework from being treated as more mature, validated, or authoritative than it currently is.

## Conceptual limitations

The distinction between a human-readable policy and an operational policy may not always be clean.

In practice, policy implementation may be distributed across:

- laws
- regulations
- guidance
- standards
- contracts
- procedures
- technical specifications
- system configurations
- professional judgement
- informal organisational practices

The second policy set may therefore exist as a distributed operational layer rather than as one single document.

## Terminology is provisional

Terms such as:

- Two-Set Policy
- operational policy
- interpretation vulnerability
- monitoring-system vulnerability
- non-automatable provision
- deployment-blocking TODO
- operational policy drift

are provisional.

Future literature review and case studies may require the terminology to be revised, narrowed, merged, or replaced.

## Novelty has not been established

The project does not currently claim that the framework or its terminology is entirely novel.

Substantial related work exists across:

- Rules as Code
- Policy as Code
- computational law
- legal informatics
- requirements engineering
- business rules
- decision modelling
- AI governance
- agent governance
- robotics safety
- administrative discretion
- sociotechnical systems
- provenance and auditability

A more comprehensive literature review may identify close prior work or stronger existing approaches.

## Policy interpretation is contested

Many policy provisions do not have one objectively correct operational interpretation.

Different authorised experts may reasonably disagree because of:

- jurisdiction
- legal doctrine
- institutional role
- policy purpose
- affected-group perspective
- risk tolerance
- evidence
- social context
- ethical position

The framework should record disagreement rather than present one interpretation as automatically correct.

## Ambiguity is not always a defect

Some policy ambiguity may be intentional or necessary.

It may preserve:

- contextual judgement
- proportionality
- professional discretion
- judicial interpretation
- flexibility for unforeseen cases
- democratic contestation
- evolving social values

The framework must distinguish harmful underspecification from legitimate discretion.

## Not all provisions should be automated

The framework does not assume that every policy statement should become machine-executable.

Some provisions may require:

- authorised human judgement
- legal interpretation
- public deliberation
- professional discretion
- institutional approval
- case-specific assessment
- an explicit prohibition on automation

## Schemas do not establish correctness

A valid JSON document or schema-compliant rule does not prove that the rule is:

- legally correct
- ethically justified
- faithful to policy
- institutionally authorised
- safe
- fair
- complete
- democratically legitimate

Technical validity, semantic validity, and normative validity are different.

## AI-assisted extraction may introduce error

AI systems used to extract or classify policy content may:

- omit relevant clauses
- invent relationships
- misclassify actors
- simplify legal meaning
- overlook exceptions
- assign incorrect authority
- treat examples as requirements
- miss implied rights or duties

Human review is therefore required.

## Human review is not automatically reliable

Human reviewers may also introduce:

- bias
- inconsistency
- incomplete analysis
- institutional self-interest
- legal error
- technical misunderstanding
- overconfidence
- domain-specific assumptions

Independent review and disagreement analysis will be necessary.

## Case-study limitations

The initial National Digital Health Blueprint case is exploratory.

It is limited by:

- selective workflow coverage
- incomplete operational extraction
- absence of official institutional validation
- absence of affected-group participation
- absence of legal adjudication
- limited empirical testing
- limited statistical analysis
- incomplete cross-document comparison

The case should not be treated as an official interpretation or complete implementation specification.

## Cross-domain generalisation is unproven

A framework that appears useful in digital health may not transfer directly to:

- transport
- finance
- education
- agriculture
- industrial robotics
- defence
- public benefits
- content moderation
- environmental regulation

Multiple cases will be required before stable generic elements can be identified.

## Jurisdictional differences

Policies from different jurisdictions may use similar terms with different legal meanings.

A common schema may improve comparison, but it may also hide important differences.

The framework should normalise representation without claiming to harmonise law.

## Redressal may not be fully representable

A structured redressal model may describe:

- complaint channels
- grievance owners
- timelines
- evidence
- appeals
- remedies

However, effective redressal also depends on:

- accessibility
- cost
- institutional independence
- enforcement power
- legal rights
- public awareness
- procedural fairness
- practical ability to obtain remedy

A schema cannot guarantee these conditions.

## Monitoring can create false assurance

Monitoring systems may produce complete logs and dashboards while failing to establish:

- legitimacy
- fairness
- meaningful consent
- proportionality
- correct authority
- public value
- effective redressal

Technical observability should not be confused with substantive compliance.

## Runtime enforcement is future work

The current repository does not include a production policy engine or enforcement service.

Future prototypes may evaluate permissions, prohibitions, evidence, authority, and escalation, but they will not automatically establish legal compliance.

## Security and misuse risks

A structured operational policy may be misused to:

- automate excessive control
- centralise authority
- weaken professional judgement
- conceal political choices
- create rigid enforcement
- enable surveillance
- produce false confidence
- legitimise unjust rules

Future versions will require threat modelling and misuse analysis.

## Participation and legitimacy

The current framework does not yet provide a validated process for determining:

- who should participate
- how affected groups are selected
- how disagreement is resolved
- who has final authority
- how dissent is preserved
- how public accountability is ensured

These are institutional and democratic questions, not only technical design questions.

## Evaluation limitations

The framework has not yet been evaluated through:

- independent expert review
- controlled experiments
- inter-rater reliability analysis
- real-world deployment
- longitudinal monitoring
- impact evaluation
- adversarial testing
- cross-jurisdiction studies

Claims should remain proportionate to the current evidence.

## Current conclusion

Two-Set Policy should currently be treated as:

- a research hypothesis
- a provisional analytical framework
- an open set of structured artefacts
- a basis for case-study development
- an invitation for critique and validation

It should not currently be treated as:

- an established theory
- a legal standard
- a compliance certification method
- a production policy engine
- a universal governance model
- a proven solution
